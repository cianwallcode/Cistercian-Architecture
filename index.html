Cistercian Architecture

The Cistercian Paradigm

An exploration of structural austerity and the 12th-century monastic reformation.

The Cistercian Order, or Ordo Cisterciensis, emphasized a return to strict adherence to the Rule of Saint Benedict. Architecturally, this manifested as a rejection of Cluniac ornamentation, favoring geometric purity, light, and the manipulation of spatial volumes to facilitate spiritual contemplation.

The diffusion of the Order followed a rigid filiation system. Each foundation maintained structural homogeneity, often utilizing the "Bernardin plan" to ensure liturgical efficiency.

Detail Analysis: Structural Morphology

Cistercian morphology is characterized by:

  • The Pointed Arch: Utilized for structural efficacy, allowing for higher, thinner walls and maximized verticality.
  • The Cloister: Defined by a square peripteral space, central to the monastic movement, acting as a transition zone between the profane and the divine.
  • Absence of Ornamentation: The intentional exclusion of figurative sculpture and stained glass, prioritizing the interplay of light on raw stone surfaces.
